# Lesson 12: Communities, Partnerships, and Affiliates

## TL;DR

The third distribution leg most engineers ignore. Communities give you free, durable distribution if you're useful (not promotional). Partnerships compound when you find non-competitive complements. Affiliates work when the math is honest and the partner's audience overlaps yours. All three are slow to start, fastest to compound.

## Core idea

```
COMMUNITY      → useful presence in 1-2 places where your buyer hangs out
PARTNERSHIP    → 1+1=3 deals with non-competitors who share your buyer
AFFILIATE      → paid distribution where the unit economics work
```

These three replace ads for solo operators. Ads cost money you don't have. These cost time and reputation, which compound.

## How it works in practice

### Communities — the right way

**Bad approach:**
- Drop a link to your product in 50 Slacks/Discords
- Post "I built X, what do you think?" in r/Entrepreneur
- DM-spam community members

**Right approach:**
- Pick **one community** where your buyer is active
- Be useful for **3 months before** mentioning your product
- Answer questions, share frameworks, help others ship
- When someone asks for your kind of solution, *then* mention it (only when relevant)
- Earn the right to promote by giving 10× value first

Where to find buyer communities:

| Buyer type | Where they live |
|---|---|
| Engineers / developers | HackerNews, /r/programming, specific tech subreddits, Discord servers for stacks |
| PMs / product people | Mind The Product Slack, Lenny's community, Reforge cohorts |
| Marketers / GTM | Demand Curve, Exit Five, GrowthMentor, RevOps Co-op |
| Founders / indie hackers | IndieHackers, Build in Public, Pioneer, Microconf alumni |
| Industry pros (CPAs, lawyers, agents) | Industry-specific subreddits, professional FB groups, LinkedIn groups |
| Designers | Designer Hangout Slack, Designer News |

**The 80/20 rule:** 80% give-value posts, 20% your-product posts. Most spammers do the inverse.

### Partnerships — the unsung distribution

A great partnership: two non-competing products that share an ICP and amplify each other.

Examples:
- Stripe + Memberful (payments + creator memberships)
- Notion + Linear (docs + ticketing for product teams)
- Substack + various analytics tools
- Cursor + Linear (recent integration, mutual promotion)

The honest partnership format:
1. **Identify**: 5 non-competing tools your buyer also uses
2. **Reach out 1-on-1** to the founder (not "BD@")
3. **Propose a specific exchange**: integration + co-marketing post + email blast to each list
4. **Measure**: signups, revenue, retention from partner-sourced users
5. **Double down on what works**

The mistake most operators make: trying to partner with brands 100× their size. You can't. Partner *across*, not up. Two solo operators with $10K MRR each can build something 10x more useful than one of them alone.

### Affiliates — the math has to be honest

An affiliate program works only when:
- Your **LTV** is high enough to pay 20-30% commission
- Your buyer is **content-influenced** (you're sold by recommendation, not pure self-discovery)
- You can **track attribution** cleanly
- You have **partners with audiences that overlap your ICP**

Bad affiliate fit:
- Low ARPU products (sub-$50/mo)
- Self-serve products with high virality (no need for affiliates)
- Products with short payback period

Good affiliate fit:
- B2B SaaS at $200-2000/mo with 1+ year retention
- Productivity / workflow tools that pros recommend
- Niche prosumer products (where one creator can move 50 customers)

Standard structure for AI products in 2026:
- 20% recurring for first 12 months
- 30% one-time for annual plans
- Cookie window: 90 days
- Self-service signup with manual review (block fraud)

Tooling: Rewardful or PartnerStack for self-service. Manual via spreadsheet for first 10 partners.

### The compounding flywheel

These three reinforce each other:

```
Communities → builds trust → attracts partners
Partners    → drive users   → some become affiliates
Affiliates  → drive users   → some become community advocates
```

After 18 months of consistent investment, this flywheel becomes your primary distribution. By month 36, it's hard for new entrants to catch up because you've built relationships they can't shortcut.

## Common traps

| Trap | Why |
|---|---|
| Joining 20 communities, being active in 0 | Pick 1, be deeply useful |
| Promoting on day 1 | Earn the right; you don't have it yet |
| "BD" emails to enterprise companies | Solo operators don't get partnership replies from F500 |
| Flat affiliate rate without doing the math | Pay too little = no signups; too much = no margin |
| Affiliate program without attribution | You're guessing |
| Treating affiliates as a launch tactic | It's a 12-month investment |

## The 12-month plan (realistic)

```
Month 1-3:   Pick community. Be useful 5 hours/week. No promotion.
Month 4-6:   Soft promotion when relevant. Build first 1-2 partner relationships.
Month 7-9:   Launch affiliate program (after $5K+ MRR). Recruit 5 partners manually.
Month 10-12: Scale what works. Drop what doesn't.

End of year 1: 10-20% of MRR from these channels.
End of year 2: 30-50% of MRR (the compounding starts).
```

## Exercise

This month, pick **one** of the three:

**If you choose community:**
- Pick one specific community (not 5)
- Make 20 substantive contributions over 30 days (no promotion)
- Track: how many people DM you organically about what you're working on?

**If you choose partnerships:**
- List 10 non-competing tools your buyer uses
- Reach out 1-on-1 to 5 founders this month
- Propose 1 specific co-marketing exchange (newsletter swap, joint webinar, integration)
- Track: how many ship?

**If you choose affiliates:**
- (Only if you have $5K+ MRR. If not, skip; come back later.)
- Set up Rewardful or PartnerStack
- Recruit 10 partners manually with a personal pitch (not blast email)
- Track: signups attributed to affiliates over 60 days
