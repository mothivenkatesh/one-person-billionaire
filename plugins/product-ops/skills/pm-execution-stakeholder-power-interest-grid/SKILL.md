# Power-Interest Grid for Stakeholder Management (Roman Pichler)

## Purpose

Most PMs over-invest in low-power / low-interest stakeholders (the noisy ones) and under-invest in high-power / low-interest ones (the dangerous ones). The Power-Interest Grid maps stakeholders onto a 2x2 so you can spend your finite political capital where it actually shifts outcomes.

This skill applies Roman Pichler's framework.

## When to Use

- Starting a new role and mapping the political terrain
- Kicking off a cross-functional initiative (launch, migration, deprecation)
- Auditing where your stakeholder time actually goes
- A senior person blocked a decision and you didn't see it coming
- A peer is escalating around you and you need to know who to brief first

## The Grid

```
                HIGH POWER
                    │
   Context Setters  │  Players
   (Keep Satisfied) │  (Manage Closely)
                    │
  ──── LOW INTEREST ┼ HIGH INTEREST ────
                    │
       Crowd        │  Subjects
      (Monitor)     │  (Keep Informed)
                    │
                LOW POWER
```

## Four Quadrants

### Players — High Power, High Interest (Manage Closely)
**Who:** Sponsors, engineering leads on critical path, key customers, the CPO/VP whose OKR you're attached to.

**Strategy:** Trustful connection. Regular 1:1s. Joint strategy workshops. Bring them into product decisions before they ask.

**Cadence:** Weekly or bi-weekly. Async-default with sync escalation when stakes spike.

### Context Setters — High Power, Low Interest (Keep Satisfied)
**Who:** Senior execs not directly involved, legal/compliance, finance, board members.

**Strategy:** Efficient, structured communication. Brief them on strategic decisions affecting their domain. Don't bury them in detail. Monitor satisfaction — they go from "satisfied" to "blocking" without warning.

**Cadence:** Monthly summaries. Async only unless they request more.

### Subjects — Low Power, High Interest (Keep Informed)
**Who:** Customer-facing teams (Sales, CS, Support), engaged users, allied PMs.

**Strategy:** Keep informed of progress. Offer feedback channels. Update on decisions that impact their work.

**Cadence:** Bi-weekly updates. Office hours. Shared roadmap doc.

### Crowd — Low Power, Low Interest (Monitor)
**Who:** Most of the org, distant teams, casual observers.

**Strategy:** General communication channels (all-hands updates, public roadmap, blog). Watch for escalation — a Crowd member who becomes interested AND gains power is the next surprise.

**Cadence:** Existing org channels. No bespoke effort.

## Process

1. **List every stakeholder by name.** Aim for 15-30. Include the surprising ones.
2. **Rate each on Power (1-5).** "Can they block, fund, fire, or escalate?"
3. **Rate each on Interest (1-5).** "Do they show up in meetings, send Slack about it, ask for updates?"
4. **Plot on the 2x2.** Use Power ≥4 as "high power", Interest ≥4 as "high interest".
5. **Assign strategy per stakeholder.** Use the quadrant strategy as default; tune for specific dynamics.
6. **Audit your last 4 weeks.** Where did your stakeholder time actually go? Mismatch = adjustment needed.
7. **Re-map quarterly.** People change roles, interest shifts, power moves with reorgs.

## Output

A stakeholder map artifact (Notion / spreadsheet / Miro) with:
- 15-30 named stakeholders, each on the grid
- Assigned strategy per stakeholder
- Owner + last-updated date
- A scheduled re-mapping (quarterly default)

## Tips

- The most expensive mistake is treating a Context Setter like Crowd. They escalate fast and quietly.
- Players need joint creation, not just informing. If your sponsor only hears about decisions, they're not actually a sponsor.
- A "Subject" who builds power (gets promoted, gets the CEO's ear) becomes a Player. Re-map.
- Don't share the grid with the stakeholders themselves. It's a working artifact, not a public position.
