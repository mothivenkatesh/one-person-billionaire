# 6-Step Strategy Implementation Checklist

## Purpose

A strategy that doesn't ship is a deck. Most strategy failures aren't at the strategy layer — they happen at implementation, where vague OKRs, unstaffed plans, and unmonitored execution quietly hollow out the work. This 6-step checklist closes the gap between approved strategy and quarter-end results.

This skill applies the framework from Institute of Product Leadership's "Implementing a Successful Strategy: A Simple Six-Step Checklist."

## When to Use

- Translating an approved strategy into a quarter of execution
- Starting a new initiative, product line, or major feature program
- A quarter ended and you missed targets — diagnosing where implementation failed
- Onboarding a new PM into an in-flight strategy
- Replacing "we have a strategy" with "we have a strategy that's being executed"

## The Six Steps

### Step 1 — Define Objectives & KPIs
Clear, measurable, time-boxed. Use OKRs to give structure. Account for context, dependencies, and whether goals are operational vs. aspirational.

**Example (good):** "Launch Acme widget in Q2 with 300 daily active users by week 8 post-launch."
**Example (bad):** "Improve user engagement."

**Failure modes:**
- Vague or unmeasurable KPIs
- Misalignment with business goals
- Too many objectives (more than 3-5 per team)

### Step 2 — Describe the Plan
Break objectives into actionable tasks. Build a roadmap or sprint plan that's specific enough for someone joining mid-quarter to pick up.

**Failure modes:**
- Unclear task breakdown
- Roles and responsibilities undefined
- Implementation details missing — "the team will figure it out"

### Step 3 — Allocate Resources
Distribute time, talent, and leadership. Confirm staffing. Align org culture and incentives. Navigate politics to secure what's needed.

**Failure modes:**
- Optimistic resource estimates ("we'll borrow a designer")
- Talent misallocation — senior people on routine work
- Misaligned organizational support — your sponsor isn't actually committing

### Step 4 — Execute the Plan
Ship work aligned with the objectives. Build MVPs with explicit post-launch plans. Stay focused on customer usage patterns, not internal milestones.

**Failure modes:**
- Feature work drifts from objectives
- No post-MVP plan — ship-and-forget
- Shipping internally without customer validation

### Step 5 — Measure KPIs
Monitor throughput, cycle time, work-in-progress AND the KPIs from Step 1. Make data-driven calls. Don't trade quality for speed.

**Failure modes:**
- Quality measurement absent
- KPIs unmonitored after week 2
- Speed prioritized to hit dates that no longer matter

### Step 6 — Adjust Strategy Based on Feedback
Listen to customers. Watch market and competitor moves. Implement continuous improvements. Adjust quickly when signals are clear; resist whipsawing on noise.

**Failure modes:**
- Feedback ignored because "the plan is the plan"
- Strategy adjustments delayed until the next planning cycle
- Adapting too fast — every Slack message becomes a pivot

## Process

1. **Run the checklist top-down at quarter start.** Each step gets explicit answers in writing.
2. **Owner per step.** Not a team — a name.
3. **Make Step 5 (Measure) visible.** A dashboard or weekly metric review where the KPIs from Step 1 actually appear.
4. **Schedule the Step 6 review.** Monthly minimum. Without a scheduled review, "adjust based on feedback" never happens.
5. **Diagnose failures by walking the checklist backwards.** If results are off, find the highest-numbered step that wasn't done well.

## Output

A strategy implementation document with:
- OKRs (Step 1) on page 1
- Plan with task breakdown and roles (Step 2)
- Staffing plan with named owners (Step 3)
- Execution updates tied to OKRs (Steps 4-5)
- A scheduled monthly adjustment review (Step 6)

## Tips

- Step 3 (resourcing) is where most "ambitious" plans die. Confirm staffing in writing before committing.
- Step 5 (measurement) is the most-skipped. Build the dashboard in week 1, not week 8.
- Step 6 (adjustment) is the most-misused. Adjust on data, not on the latest stakeholder message.
- If you can't answer all 6 steps in a single page, the strategy isn't ready for execution.
