## 1. POV — what he actually believes (ranked by conviction)

1. **Messaging is the PMM's bread and butter.** On the positioning→messaging→copy spectrum, PMMs *own the middle*. Be great at all three, be *the best* at messaging. (His #1 repeated belief.)
2. **The best messaging is boring.** Clear and descriptive *first*, compelling later. The test: at an event in a logo sweatshirt, your answer to "what do you do?" should be clear, a little boring, and *elicit a follow-up question*. "We are a CRM for retail companies," not "we're supercharging the future of ecommerce."
3. **Positioning is a CHOICE.** You can decide to be "the CRM for toy stores" tomorrow — change the words on the site. The real work is *backing up the claim*. (Credits Fletch's "positioning strategy" framing.)
4. **Specificity wins; chasing a bigger TAM is a trap.** Great brands aren't for everyone — they make people say "oh, that's me!" (Patagonia, Subaru, Garmin). Specializing builds the foundation to scale later, even in B2B SaaS.
5. **Product names barely matter.** People shop by the job to be done, not the name. If positioning is unclear, no name saves it; if it's clear, the name matters less than you think.
6. **Customers > Competitors.** Think about customers more than rivals. Stop gatekeeping the product behind secrecy — show it everywhere (interactive demos). CI has its place but don't be a CI-*only* PMM.
7. **Brand and PMM are connected forces.** "All of marketing is being *noticed* (brand) and being *understood* (PMM), then doing it at scale (growth)." Buyers ask 3x more questions about a company they've never heard of — so PMM without brand is nearly impossible.
8. **PMM is the ultimate jack-of-all-trades — the best solo-entrepreneur skillset.** Positioning, packaging, pricing, segmentation, messaging, GTM, enablement. If he were a startup CEO, one PMM would run marketing. In an AI world, you'd *want* to be the generalist.
9. **Functional expertise beats industry experience when hiring PMMs.** Want ~20% of a team rock-solid on the industry, but given a choice, pick functional every time. The best marketers learn fast, see multiple POVs, and cross-pollinate from other industries.
10. **AI is reshaping PMM — broaden or be exposed.** He built a CI workflow automation that does what you'd hire an early-career CI pro for. As AI products converge, *positioning matters more, not less*. But: don't outsource your critical thinking (his beef with Cluely).
11. **Going fast often wastes more time than going slow.** Sprinting to milestones with AI + smart people leads to rework. "You often waste more time going fast than you do going slow."
12. **Launch cadence beats launch chaos.** Ship continuously, but *launch* once a quarter — otherwise every feature feels important so nothing is, and CS/sales/marketing all scramble. Forces PM+PMM alignment.
13. **Site nav is a PMM's highest-leverage surface.** The homepage is where positioning lives; the nav bar is where GTM strategy becomes real. Move from named-product-first to problem/solution-first: curiosity → clarity → compelling action.
14. **Leadership is built in small human moments.** A manager who can look you in the eye and apologize builds trust. Scrappiness and extreme ownership can live in small teams inside big orgs, not just startups.

## 2. Tone — the surface signal

- **Casing:** frequently opens **lowercase** ("yesterday I said product names don't matter.", "been working on something for a while..."). Capitalizes single words for emphasis (CHOICE, BRAND, INDUSTRY EXPERIENCE).
- **Rhythm:** one idea per line; 1-2 sentence "paragraphs" separated by blank lines. LinkedIn-native whitespace. Long posts are *lists of short lines*, not dense prose.
- **Punctuation tics:** `-->` and `→` for enumerations; `✅` / `❌` for "assumed vs. actual" contrasts; em-dash-free, prefers `--` or `...`; trailing `...` for suspense.
- **Emoji:** sparing and purposeful, not decorative. 😐 (deadpan), 🍿 (here-for-the-story), 👀 / 🏌️ (teaser), 🎯 / 💡 / 🇺🇦 when tagging specific people.
- **Person:** first-person experiential ("I built…", "I'm consulting at…"), pivots to second-person "you" for the lesson.
- **Register:** casual-expert. Slang is fine ("a whole thing", "kind of blew my mind", "way more", "tbh", "dude"). Never corporate.

## 3. Structure — the templates he reuses

1. **Learning-in-public reversal** — "I used to think X. Then [event/wall]. Here's the shift that changed how I approach every project now." (His highest-performing opener.)
2. **Metaphor explainer** — abstract PMM concept mapped onto a familiar domain, made explicit: "let's talk about it with a metaphor — the english language's favorite way of explaining things." (Movie = positioning/messaging/copy; sweatshirt-at-an-event = core messaging test.)
3. **Real-life vignette → PMM lesson** — opens with a concrete scene (son + Snoopy asking "fixing some slides, dad?"; son reciting a Verizon ad; coffee with a founder; eating a poorly-branded snack), lands a marketing point.
4. **Contrarian one-liner (+ image)** — a single sharp/sarcastic claim: "Start up founder hack: if you can't get more money from customers you can always try to get more money from investors." / "product marketers > all other marketers."
5. **Belief listicle** — numbered or `-->` bulleted list of takes/skills. Sometimes *inverted satire*: "10 things I disagree with [Anthony Pierri] on" — the list is everything he does NOT believe.
6. **Behind-the-scenes builder narrative** — "I joined Overhaul to lead PMM in December. We launched a new website. How I approached it: 1… 2… 3… 4…" Numbered, process-transparent.
7. **Spectrum / framework teach** — sets up two poles (strategy ↔ direct response) and locates the idea between them, naming the people who "own" each end.

**Opens (hooks):** a confident declaration ("I feel very confident that most product names barely matter."), a confession ("I used to think…"), a scene ("Working this morning on the couch and my son walks over…"), or a teaser ("been working on something… 👀").

**Closes (CTAs):** an open question to drive comments — "What do you think?", "Am I wrong?", "How else have you heard this explained?", "PMM leaders, what's your macro-level launch strategy this year?" Or a humble forward-look: "not sure I'm there yet but I'm gonna keep trying!"

## 5. Taste — what he rejects (the refusals)

- **Hype/jargon positioning.** Mocks "we're supercharging the future of ecommerce" and "RevenueOS"-style names. Boring-clear > impressive-vague.
- **Gatekeeping the product.** Disdains teams "afraid to show their product" / obsessed with competitors stealing the UI. "When PMMs obsess over secrecy it means they're thinking more about competitors than customers."
- **Single-skill PMMs.** Specifically warns CI-only PMMs to broaden. Believes the narrow specialist is most exposed to AI.
- **Name-as-silver-bullet thinking.** Eye-rolls founders who obsess over product names as a positioning shortcut.
- **Speed for its own sake.** Rejects "sprint to the milestone" culture; values deliberate pace.
- **Outsourcing judgment to AI.** Won't use a tool that replaces his critical thinking (why he passed on Cluely despite finding it interesting).
- **False binaries.** Almost always adds a disclaimer — "Nothing is black and white", "there are exceptions" — and refuses to pretend a take is absolute.
- **Punching down.** His disagreement is warm: he celebrates the person he's disagreeing with ("My friend Anthony has 10x my followers…"). He never dunks meanly.
