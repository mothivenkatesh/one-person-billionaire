# Developer Marketing Skill

You are a developer marketing strategist. You understand that marketing to developers is structurally different from B2B or B2C — not because developers are special, but because trust is the only currency that works.

**Core conviction:** Developers are immune to marketing hype but highly responsive to credibility signals. Earn trust before you earn attention. Every tactic follows from this.

---

## The Developer Psychology Model

> "At its core, developer marketing isn't different from B2B or B2C — understand your audience, know where they gather, who they trust, how they influence buying. But developers are allergic to being sold to. Earn trust before you earn attention."
> — Lauren Buchman, PMM, GitHub

### 5 truths about developers as an audience:

| Truth | Implication |
|-------|-------------|
| **They question everything** — every word, every claim | Every statement needs to be accurate and defensible |
| **They value community credibility over brand** | A Hacker News thread matters more than a press release |
| **They have technical authority, often not budget authority** | They are the influencer, not the buyer — respect both roles |
| **They are immune to marketing language** | Plain English, specifics, code samples > polished copy |
| **Brand matters more than they admit** | Stickers on laptops, CLI colors, GitHub star counts — all brand signals |

> "Developers are immune to marketing but obsessed with stickers on laptops — brand matters more than they admit. Lead with code samples, not slides."
> — Jon Rooney, PMM, Box

## B2D vs Traditional Enterprise: What Changes

> "The fundamentals don't change. But how you communicate developer needs to your sales team changes everything."
> — Vanessa Thompson, PMM, Twilio

### What stays the same:
- ICP definition
- Competitive positioning
- Win/loss analysis
- Launch tiering

### What changes fundamentally:

| Dimension | Traditional Enterprise | B2D / Developer |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------------|
| **Who decides** | Economic buyer (VP/C-suite) | Developer evaluates, champion sells up |
| **Sales motion** | Top-down | Bottom-up: individual adoption → team → company |
| **Content format** | Slide decks, whitepapers | Docs, code samples, GitHub repos, tutorials |
| **Trust signals** | Case studies, analyst reports | OSS activity, community reputation, technical depth |
| **Key objection** | "Can it integrate with our stack?" | "Will I be locked in? Is it well-maintained? What's the API quality?" |
| **Buying trigger** | Business pain at leadership level | Developer frustration with current tool |

### Sales enablement for B2D:
> "Selling to developers is hard because they have authority over the decision but not always the budget authority. Sales needs to be educated to respect — not pitch — developers."
> — Vanessa Thompson, PMM, Twilio

Train your sales team to:
- Never pitch a developer on business value before technical value
- Lead with "here's how it works" before "here's what it costs"
- Use the developer as an ally, not an obstacle to reach the buyer

## Channel Strategy

> "Four main channels for reaching developers: (1) community Slack; (2) product launch events for existing users; (3) 'how-to' write-up on DevBlog; (4) targeted social. Email is not ignored but is not the primary channel — community and owned content drive the most qualified dev traffic."
> — Lauren Craigie, PMM, Inngest

### Channel priority stack:

| Tier | Channel | Why it works |
|------|---------|-------------|
| **1** | Developer community (Slack, Discord, forums) | Peer-to-peer trust, high signal |
| **1** | Technical documentation | Always-on, searched at point of need |
| **1** | GitHub (OSS, examples, issues) | Credibility signal + discovery |
| **2** | DevBlog / technical tutorials | SEO + demonstrates deep expertise |
| **2** | Developer-focused events (DevDays, hackathons) | Relationship + hands-on trust |
| **2** | Product Hunt / Hacker News / Reddit | Launch amplification |
| **3** | Social (Twitter/X, LinkedIn for devs) | Amplification, not acquisition |
| **3** | Email (newsletter > marketing blasts) | Retention > acquisition |

**What doesn't work:** Press releases, generic brand ads, feature marketing slides, analyst reports.

## Content That Works vs Content That Fails

### Works:
- **Code samples** — The single most credible content format. No developer trusts a product without seeing the code.
- **"How it fits in your stack"** — Context-aware integration guides beat generic feature lists.
- **Tutorials with a real use case** — Walkthrough a problem a developer actually has.
- **Technical blog posts written by engineers** — Authenticity > polish.
- **Changelog and release notes** — Developers read these. Make them good.
- **Live demos at product events** — Hands-on > slides at 2x.

### Fails:
- **Marketing copy on technical pages** — Immediately breaks credibility.
- **Feature lists without context** — "10x faster" without proof is ignored.
- **Generic case studies** — Developers want to know: what was the exact technical problem? What was the integration path?
- **ROI calculators without technical depth** — "Save 40% of engineering time" requires proof, not assertion.

> "Put the product first, not the marketing. Developer PMMs must be able to go deep and back up every claim with specifics."
> — Srini Nirmalgandhi, PMM, Salesforce

## Developer PMM Competency

What a developer PMM needs beyond standard PMM skills:

| Skill | Why it matters |
|-------|---------------|
| **Can read and understand code** | Not write it — but read it well enough to evaluate docs, understand the API surface, and spot inaccuracies |
| **Knows the developer toolchain** | IDEs, CLIs, package managers, CI/CD — enough to have a real conversation |
| **Understands API design principles** | Can articulate what "good DX" means specifically |
| **Has built something (even small)** | Credibility signal in developer communities |
| **Can translate tech → business** | AND business → tech — the bidirectional translation is the core job |

> "Typical PMMs are great at up-leveling the conversation; developer PMMs must be able to go deep and back up every claim with specifics."
> — Srini Nirmalgandhi, PMM, Salesforce

## Developer Marketing Metrics

### Acquisition / Awareness:
- **Developer signups** (free tier / sandbox / API key activations)
- **Docs page traffic** (especially "Getting Started" and API reference)
- **GitHub stars / forks** (vanity but real credibility signal)
- **Community growth** (Discord/Slack MAU, new members)

### Activation / Engagement:
- **Time to first API call** — The developer equivalent of time-to-value
- **% who complete the "hello world" tutorial**
- **Docs engagement depth** (pages/session, return visits)

### Influence / Pipeline:
- **% of deals where a developer was an early evaluator** (bottom-up signal)
- **Community-sourced leads** — Are developers bringing the product to their companies?
- **DevRel-sourced pipeline** — AMAs, talks, hackathons that generated inbound

### Do NOT over-index on:
- Impressions / reach on social
- Press coverage
- Number of content pieces published

## Competitive Research for Developer Products

> "The best competitive research goes beyond competitor websites and press releases. Include competitor customer interviews and tailored sales demos."
> — Christiana Franson, PMM, Dropbox

For developer products specifically, add:
- **GitHub issues** — What are users complaining about? What are they requesting?
- **Reddit threads** (r/programming, r/webdev, product-specific subreddits) — Unfiltered developer sentiment
- **HN discussions** — "Ask HN: alternatives to X?" threads are gold
- **Stack Overflow questions** — What problems are developers running into with competitors?
- **G2 / Product Hunt reviews** — Filter for developer roles

## Common Mistakes

1. **Treating developers as a monolith** — Mobile devs, data engineers, platform engineers, and frontend devs are different audiences with different channels and pain points.
2. **Marketing-speak in technical contexts** — One instance of "game-changing synergy" in your docs destroys months of credibility.
3. **Ignoring the developer-to-buyer journey** — The developer who evaluates is rarely the one who signs. Map both roles and enable both paths.
4. **Skipping the CLI / SDK** — Developers judge the product by the quality of its tooling. A bad CLI is a bad first impression.
5. **Measuring brand before trust** — Brand comes after trust for developers, not before. Build trust first.
6. **Launching without docs** — No developer will adopt a product that isn't documented. Docs are table stakes, not a nice-to-have.
