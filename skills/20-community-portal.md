# Skill 20: The Community Portal — Building Your Central Landing Page

**Category:** Community Building | **Phase:** 3. Formalize

> **Key Principle:** Even with a decentralized community, you need one central page that tells people where everything is. This is your front door. Without it, most users will never discover your community exists.

## What It Is

A single, well-organized landing page that serves as the entry point for anyone who wants to get involved in your community. It centralizes all programs, groups, resources, events, and guidelines in one place, even if the actual communities are spread across dozens of platforms.

## How Ben Did It at Notion

Notion built notion.community as the central hub. Even though all the actual community groups were decentralized across Facebook, Discord, Telegram, Reddit, WeChat, and dozens of other platforms, this one page told users everything they needed to know.

Without this page, most users would never discover the community exists unless they stumbled across it organically. The portal solved the discovery problem for decentralized communities.

## Step-by-Step SOP

### Step 1 — Choose Your URL
- Ideal: yourproduct.community or community.yourproduct.com
- This becomes the canonical URL you reference everywhere
- It should be memorable and easy to share verbally

### Step 2 — Design the Page Structure

Sections to include:
- **Welcome and overview** — What is this community? Who is it for?
- **How to get involved** — Clear paths for different types of participation
- **Community groups directory** — List of all groups by language, region, interest, platform
- **Ambassador program** — What it is, how to apply, what you get
- **Certification program** — How to become certified, link to directory
- **Events** — Upcoming events, how to host your own
- **Champions community** — For enterprise customers, how to join
- **Code of conduct** — Behavioral expectations, reporting process
- **Brand guidelines** — Assets and rules for community use of your brand
- **Resources** — Templates, guides, tutorials created by the community

### Step 3 — Make Discovery Effortless
- Link to the portal from your main website navigation
- Include it in onboarding emails and in-product messages
- Reference it in support documentation
- Share it on social media regularly

### Step 4 — Use Your Own Product (If Possible)
Notion used Notion itself to build their community page. This served double duty:
- Showcased Notion's capabilities to prospective users
- Made updates easy (the team could edit without engineering)
- If your product can be used this way, it's a powerful dogfooding signal

### Step 5 — Keep It Updated
- Assign someone to update the portal at least monthly
- Add new community groups as they form
- Update event listings
- Refresh featured content and resources
- An outdated portal is worse than no portal

## What Makes a Great Community Portal

- **One page, not a whole website.** Keep it simple and scannable.
- **Clear calls to action.** Every section should answer: what do I do next?
- **Multiple entry points.** Different paths for different levels of involvement.
- **Disclaimers where needed.** Make clear which groups are community-run vs. company-run.
- **Social proof.** Show numbers, testimonials, featured community members.
