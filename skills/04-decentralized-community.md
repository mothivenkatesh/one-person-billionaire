# Skill 4: Decentralized Community Architecture

**Category:** Community Building | **Phase:** 4. Scale

> **Key Principle:** Let people create their own communities on any platform. Support and fund them, but don't own or moderate them. Authenticity and global reach outweigh the loss of control.

## What It Is

Deliberately choosing NOT to centralize your community into one platform you control. Instead, you empower dozens (or hundreds) of independent groups worldwide, on whatever platform the local community prefers.

## How Ben Did It at Notion

Notion faced a clear choice: build one central community hub, or let communities form wherever they wanted. They chose decentralization. The result was dozens of groups across 50+ languages on WeChat, Telegram, Facebook, Reddit, Discord, Kakao, and every other local platform. Notion didn't moderate or admin ANY of these groups. Ben wasn't even an admin in them.

## Step-by-Step SOP

### Step 1 — Let It Happen Organically
- Don't create the groups yourself
- Watch for people starting Facebook groups, Discord servers, Telegram channels, subreddits, etc. around your product
- This is your signal that decentralized community has organic demand

### Step 2 — Support, Don't Own
- You are NOT the admin of these groups
- The community runs them entirely
- Make this clear publicly on your community page: "These are community-run groups"
- Include disclaimers so expectations are set correctly

### Step 3 — Provide Guardrails
- Publish a clear Code of Conduct on your community page
- Share best practices and moderation guidelines with group leaders
- Build strong personal relationships with moderators and group leaders
- Fold active group leaders into your Ambassador Program

### Step 4 — Create a Central Directory
- Build a page that lists ALL community groups (e.g., notion.community)
- Organize by language, region, interest area, and platform
- Direct users from your main website to this directory so they discover groups exist
- Without this directory, most users would never find these groups organically

### Step 5 — Track at Scale
- Build or use a tracking tool to monitor 100+ groups monthly
- Track for each group: member count, growth rate, activity level
- Group the data by: region, language, platform
- Outsource the data entry if needed (Ben's team did this)
- Use this data to identify which regions are growing, which need support

## Trade-Off Analysis

**Pros of Decentralized:**
- Authentic and bottoms-up (people feel ownership)
- Available in more languages, more international/global
- Higher potential for virality and faster growth
- Each community adapts to local culture and platform preferences
- Much less operational burden on your team

**Cons of Decentralized:**
- Very limited data on what's happening across groups
- Less brand control and potential brand risk
- Hard to measure overall community health
- Can't easily communicate with all community members at once
- Quality varies significantly between groups

## When This Works

This approach ONLY works if people genuinely love your product enough to create and run communities independently. If you have to force it, go centralized instead. The litmus test: are people already creating groups without you asking them to?

## Key Insight from Ben

Notion's philosophy was that these communities being bottoms-up driven was more compelling than having a tops-down approach. The strong relationship with moderators and ambassadors who ran these groups was the most feasible way to grow them in a healthy way at scale.
