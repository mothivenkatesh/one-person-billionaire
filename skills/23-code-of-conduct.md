# Skill 23: Code of Conduct & Moderation at Scale

**Category:** Operations | **Phase:** 3. Formalize

> **Key Principle:** When you don't control the communities, your code of conduct is your only enforcement tool. Write it clearly, publish it everywhere, reference it constantly, and build strong relationships with the moderators who enforce it.

## What It Is

A framework for maintaining quality and safety across a decentralized community where you don't control the platforms, don't moderate the groups, and often can't even read the conversations (because they happen in 50+ languages).

## How Ben Did It at Notion

Notion's approach was built on a simple reality: they couldn't moderate hundreds of groups across dozens of languages. Instead, they created a clear code of conduct, made it maximally visible, and invested heavily in relationships with the moderators and ambassadors who actually enforced it.

## Step-by-Step SOP

### Step 1 — Write the Code of Conduct

It should cover:
- Expected behavior (respect, constructiveness, good faith)
- Prohibited behavior (spam, harassment, discrimination, self-promotion abuse)
- Consequences for violations (warning, removal, ban)
- How to report violations (clear reporting mechanism)
- Scope: applies to ALL community spaces including third-party groups that use your brand
- Keep the language simple and direct. Avoid legalese.

### Step 2 — Publish It Everywhere
- Feature it prominently on your community portal page
- Include it in ambassador program onboarding materials
- Require acknowledgment from every ambassador and group moderator
- Ask group moderators to pin it in their communities
- Reference it in event guidelines
- Include it in any sponsorship or funding agreements
- The more places it appears, the harder it is for bad actors to claim ignorance

### Step 3 — Train Your Moderators
- Your ambassadors and group moderators are your enforcement layer
- Host sessions on how to handle common situations (spam, conflict, harassment)
- Share best practices from moderators who handle issues well
- Create a private channel where moderators can ask for guidance
- Empower them to act. They should not need your permission for every decision.

### Step 4 — Build the Reporting Pipeline
- Make it easy for community members to report issues (form, email, or in-platform)
- Ensure reports reach someone who can act
- Respond to reports promptly, even if just acknowledgment
- For severe violations, have an escalation path ready

### Step 5 — Enforce Consistently
- Reference the specific code of conduct provision when acting
- Apply consequences consistently regardless of the person's status or contribution
- Document actions taken for your own records
- For decentralized groups: communicate with the moderator and support their decision

### Step 6 — Add Disclaimers
- On your community portal, make clear which groups are community-run vs company-run
- Disclaim responsibility for content in third-party groups while maintaining standards
- This protects your brand while acknowledging the decentralized reality

## The Moderation Paradox

The more decentralized your community, the more important your code of conduct becomes. You have less direct control, so the shared values document is your primary lever. But it only works if moderators feel connected to you and aligned with your values — which is why the ambassador relationships are so critical.
