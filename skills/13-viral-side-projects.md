# Skill 13: Viral Side Project Creation (The Humus Day Formula)

**Category:** Career & Personal | **Phase:** Framework

> **Key Principle:** Build something lightweight in hours that taps into cultural resonance. Don't try to control it. Low effort input plus cultural resonance equals viral spread and ongoing momentum without maintenance.

## What It Is

A pattern for creating lightweight side projects that go viral by tapping into cultural moments or universal human interests. These projects take hours to build, not months, and can generate outsized attention, connections, and opportunities.

## Ben's Three Case Studies

### Case 1: International Humus Day
Ben bought hummusday.com and built a simple website declaring May 13th as International Humus Day. He made up the date entirely. He forgot about it.

A month later, someone included it in a vegan newsletter. From there it went viral on Twitter. Celebrities started sharing it. Every year since, it has grown bigger. Now there are humus festivals in Dubai, coverage in GQ and Cosmopolitan, and the day is listed on every "national day" calendar website as if it's a real holiday.

Sabra (the humus company) sponsored it for a few years, then tried to buy it. Ben felt they lowballed him and said no. Sabra then launched "National Humus Day" on the same date, trying to hijack it. They eventually stopped.

Total time invested: approximately 2 hours. The project runs itself with about 2 hours per year of maintenance.

### Case 2: Corona Crush
During COVID lockdowns, Ben and friends started a Facebook group where people could post about their single friends. It went viral within Jewish communities. The group now has 25,000 members and at least 100 couples have gotten married through it (likely many more).

### Case 3: Notion Wedding and Home Template
Ben shared how he and his wife use Notion to manage their household. The screenshot went viral. Some people loved it, some made fun of it. He leaned into the attention, published more templates, started a YouTube channel about it, and landed coverage in Business Insider and the New York Times.

## The Pattern

### Step 1 — Spot a Gap Nobody Is Filling
- Is there a cultural moment with no owner? (No food holidays for humus)
- Is there a social need not being met? (No dating during COVID)
- Is there a relatable experience nobody talks about publicly? (Managing a household with tech)

### Step 2 — Build Something in Hours, Not Months
- A website (hummusday.com)
- A Facebook group (Corona Crush)
- A tweet with a screenshot (Notion home template)
- Keep it dead simple. No engineering. No design sprints. Just ship it.

### Step 3 — Make It Shareable and Participatory
- People should be able to contribute, not just consume
- Humus Day: eat humus and share photos
- Corona Crush: post about your single friends
- Notion templates: duplicate and customize

### Step 4 — Don't Try to Control It
- Let it take on a life of its own
- Don't over-manage the narrative
- If people make fun of it, lean into the attention
- The virality comes from people engaging with it in their own way

### Step 5 — Let Serendipity Decide
- Some projects go nowhere. That's fine.
- The ones that work can create disproportionate impact relative to effort
- The key is volume: keep launching small things and see what sticks

## Key Insight from Ben

You can make an impact pretty easily. A lot of people think it requires years and years of effort. Humus Day was a 2-hour project. The lesson is that if you do something creative and find your own niche, you can really do something impactful. Most people are just afraid of taking the shot.
