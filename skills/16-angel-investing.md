# Skill 16: Angel Investing as Relationship Building

**Category:** Investing | **Phase:** Framework

> **Key Principle:** Small checks, big value-add. Build the relationship before investing. Deliver customers and introductions first. Use your community network as your competitive advantage.

## What It Is

An approach to angel investing where the investment is secondary to the relationship and value you provide. Instead of competing on check size, compete on helpfulness.

## How Ben Does It

Ben raised a $6M micro fund after years of learning through apprenticeship. He started by helping another angel investor (Clark) with deal sourcing and evaluation for several years before investing on his own.

His approach: small checks (around $100K), never lead rounds, add disproportionate value relative to check size. With approximately 50-60 investments to date, he focuses on being the investor founders want in their round because of what he brings, not how much he writes.

## Ben's Value-Add Stack

### 1. Recruiting Early Hires
Through Next Play, Ben has a growing database of talented operators, engineers, and executives who are actively looking for their next role.

### 2. Finding Co-Founders
The Next Play network surfaces people who want to start things. Ben can facilitate co-founder matching for portfolio companies.

### 3. Getting Early Users and Customers
Ben's background in community building means he understands how to get early traction. In some cases, he's delivered customers before investing.

### 4. Community Strategy Advice
For companies where community-led growth is relevant, Ben brings direct operational experience from scaling Notion's community from zero to a global movement.

## Step-by-Step SOP: Breaking Into Angel Investing

### Step 1 — Apprentice First
- Find an experienced angel investor who needs help
- Offer to source deals, do research, take notes, manage their pipeline
- Learn by observing their decision-making process
- This lets you learn the craft without risking your own money

### Step 2 — Start with Tiny Checks
- Once you're ready, start investing small amounts alongside your mentor or other leads
- Co-invest in deals where you have an existing relationship with the founder
- Don't try to lead rounds at the beginning

### Step 3 — Build Your Value-Add Before Your Portfolio
- Create assets and communities that make you valuable to founders
- Ben built Next Play, which serves double duty as a community AND a talent/deal pipeline
- Your value-add should be real and operational, not just "I can make introductions"

### Step 4 — Invest in What You Know
- Ben invests in areas where his community-building experience is relevant
- Look for signals he knows are meaningful: organic user love, community formation, bottoms-up growth

### Step 5 — Relationship Before Check
- Build the relationship with the founder before trying to invest
- Deliver value first: customer intros, advice, connections, time
- The investment should feel like a natural extension of an existing relationship
- Don't compete on check size. Compete on helpfulness.

## Key Insight from Ben

When asked why a founder should take his money, Ben landed on the truth: he's a builder at heart, he has empathy for people starting companies, and the things he works on (Next Play, community building, recruiting) directly help the companies he invests in. The competitive advantage isn't financial. It's operational.
